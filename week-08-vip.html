High Energy

The Power 15

5 exercises, 3 rounds, 1 minute each. Hit every major muscle group in 15 minutes flat.

  • Squat to press (dumbbells or bodyweight)
  • Push-ups (knees are fine)
  • Reverse lunges (alternating)
  • Plank hold
  • Mountain climbers

When you've got energy, use it. No gym, no excuses, your living room floor.

Medium Energy

The Steady State

15-minute brisk walk (outside if possible) followed by 5 minutes of gentle stretching.

  • 15 min brisk walk with intention
  • Hip flexor stretch (1 min each side)
  • Chest opener stretch (1 min)
  • Hamstring stretch (1 min each side)

A 15-minute walk with intention does more for your metabolism than a 45-minute gym session you dread. Movement is a signal, not a punishment.

Low Energy / Too Tired

The Recovery 10

10 minutes of gentle mobility. For the day after a double. The day you barely slept. The day your body is screaming.

  • Cat-cow (1 min)
  • Child's pose (2 min)
  • Supine twist (2 min each side)
  • Legs up the wall (3 min)

This isn't lazy. This is recovery. And recovery IS the workout.

Night Shift Note

Timing matters. Don't work out within 2 hours of trying to sleep (it raises cortisol). If you're on nights, your best window is before your shift (even 15 minutes) or on your off days. The "Recovery 10" is always safe regardless of timing.

Your One Thing This Week

If you can only do ONE thing this week: take a 15-minute walk. That's it. Outside if you can. Around the block. Around the hospital. Just move for 15 minutes with intention.

VIP Deep Dive

Why 15 Minutes Actually Works

Research from the European Journal of Applied Physiology shows that 15 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise triggers the same acute cortisol regulation as 45 minutes. For shift workers with chronically elevated cortisol, shorter bouts are actually BETTER than long sessions because long intense workouts add more cortisol to an already overloaded system.

Here's the part most fitness programs get wrong: they treat exercise as a calorie-burning tool. For nurses, the real value of movement is hormonal signaling. When you move for 15 minutes at moderate intensity, your body releases myokines (muscle-derived chemical messengers) that reduce inflammation, improve insulin sensitivity, and regulate cortisol production. You're not burning off last night's pizza. You're sending your nervous system a message: "We're safe. We can recover."

The goal is to send a movement signal, not to punish your body into submission. That's why matching workout intensity to your energy level is the most shift-proof approach possible. A Recovery 10 on a post-double day does more good than a Power 15 you had to force yourself through while running on 4 hours of sleep.

VIP Challenge

The Energy Match Challenge

For 5 workout days this week, match your workout to your energy level using the three options from this card. Before you start, rate your energy: High, Medium, or Low. Then pick the matching workout. No overriding. No guilt. Trust the system.

Log which level you picked each day and how you felt after (better, same, or worse). At the end of 5 days, look at the pattern. Most nurses find they feel better more consistently when they stop forcing high-intensity on low-energy days.
